# Mental Models — Focus, Time, Identity

These are PG's essays on how to think and live, not how to run a startup.
They are some of the most-quoted of his pieces because the same models
work outside of startups too.

## Core essays

### "Life is Short" (2016)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/vb.html
- **Thesis:** Life is shorter than you think — not in some abstract
  sense, but in the concrete number of times you'll see the people you
  love or do the things you care about. The right response is not panic
  but ruthless removal of bullshit.
- **When this applies:** User is stuck in a job/situation they hate and
  rationalizing why they can't leave, or wasting time on conflicts they
  could simply exit.
- **Related:** The Top Idea in Your Mind, Keep Your Identity Small

### "Keep Your Identity Small" (2009)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/identity.html
- **Thesis:** The more things you make part of your identity, the
  dumber you become about them. You can't think clearly about a topic
  once your sense of self depends on a position. Defense: keep your
  identity small.
- **When this applies:** User is stuck in a tribal argument (politics,
  framework wars, ideology), or can't update their views on something
  because "that's who they are."
- **Related:** What You Can't Say

### "The Top Idea in Your Mind" (2010)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/top.html
- **Thesis:** *(Cross-listed with high-agency.md.)* Whatever sits at
  the top of your mind is what your subconscious works on. You only
  get one slot at a time. Money worries, fights, romantic preoccupations
  — these crowd out everything else.
- **When this applies:** User can't focus and you suspect the reason is
  a different anxiety occupying the top slot.
- **Related:** Disconnecting Distraction, Life is Short

### "How You Know" (2014)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/know.html
- **Thesis:** Most of what you "know" you have forgotten the source of.
  Reading widely is valuable even though you can't recall most of it,
  because the shape of how you think is being formed even when the
  facts fade.
- **When this applies:** User questions whether reading/learning is
  "worth it" if they forget most of it, or feels guilty about not
  retaining things.
- **Related:** How to Do Great Work

### "Earnestness" (2020)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/earnest.html
- **Thesis:** Earnestness — caring about something genuinely, without
  irony or self-protective distance — is rarer than it looks and is a
  prerequisite for doing important work. Most adults learn to hide it.
  Don't.
- **When this applies:** User is treating their work or ambition with
  protective irony ("haha, my little startup"), or feels embarrassed
  to be openly ambitious.
- **Related:** How to Do Great Work, What You Can't Say

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Time is a hard budget, not a soft one.** Acting like it's
   infinite is the most expensive mistake.
2. **Identity is a tax on thinking.** Anything you make part of "who
   you are" becomes a topic you can't think clearly about.
3. **The top idea slot is finite and chosen.** Choose deliberately or
   it gets chosen for you.
4. **Earnestness is a competitive advantage.** Most adults have lost
   it. Keep it.

## How to apply

- When the user is stuck and the situation seems exitable: ask, *what
  would you do if you fully internalized that life is short?*
- When the user is locked into a tribal position: ask, *what would
  someone who isn't in your tribe say about this?* Keep their identity
  small.
- When the user is shrinking from open ambition: ask why, then
  reference Earnestness. They don't have to broadcast it, but they
  shouldn't hide it from themselves.
