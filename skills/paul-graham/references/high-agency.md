# High Agency, Ambition, What to Work On

PG's writing on high agency is mostly about a feedback loop: ambition is
shaped by environment, environment is shaped by who you choose to be
around, and what you actually do is downstream of what idea occupies the
top spot in your head.

## Core essays

### "How to Do Great Work" (2023)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/greatwork.html
- **Thesis:** Great work is the intersection of natural aptitude, deep
  interest, and scope to do important work. You find it by working on
  many things, noticing which ones pull you, and then committing harder
  than is comfortable.
- **When this applies:** User asks "what should I work on?", "how do I
  find my calling?", "I'm 25 and lost," "I want to do something
  important." This is the master essay on the topic — pull it first.
- **Related:** Cities and Ambition, Determination

### "Cities and Ambition" (2008)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/cities.html
- **Thesis:** Cities transmit ambition through the people in them. Each
  city sends a message about what it values — money, power, fashion,
  intellect, startups. You absorb that message whether you want to or not.
- **When this applies:** User asks about moving cities, feeling stuck in
  their environment, why they're not as ambitious as they want to be, or
  whether they should move to SF/NYC.
- **Related:** What You Can't Say

### "What You Can't Say" (2004)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/say.html
- **Thesis:** Every era has things that are obviously true but socially
  forbidden to say. The interesting question — and a useful exercise in
  independent thinking — is figuring out what those things are *now*. Test:
  what would have been heresy 100 years ago that's normal now? What's
  the inverse?
- **When this applies:** User asks about independent thinking, contrarian
  ideas, finding ideas no one is working on, or sensing they're inside a
  bubble.
- **Related:** Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas, Earnestness

### "Disconnecting Distraction" (2008)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/distraction.html
- **Thesis:** The most insidious distractions are the ones that look like
  work. The defense is structural: separate the machine you use for real
  work from the machine you use for everything else.
- **When this applies:** User says they can't focus, are addicted to
  Twitter/news, or feel busy without making progress.
- **Related:** The Top Idea in Your Mind, Maker's Schedule

### "The Top Idea in Your Mind" (2010)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/top.html
- **Thesis:** You only get one or two top ideas at a time. Whatever sits
  at the top is what your mind drifts to in the shower, on a walk, at 3am.
  Choose it deliberately, because everything below the top idea gets
  starved.
- **When this applies:** User is doing too many things, has a side project
  draining a main project, can't stop thinking about a fight/conflict, or
  is unclear on priorities.
- **Related:** Disconnecting Distraction, Life is Short

### "How to Lose Time and Money" (2010)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/selfindulgence.html
- **Thesis:** People lose time and money not through obvious sins but
  through things that don't feel indulgent — bureaucracy, prestige
  signaling, optimizing the wrong metric. The dangerous category is
  "respectable distractions."
- **When this applies:** User is justifying low-leverage work because it
  looks legitimate, or stuck in something prestigious that isn't moving
  them forward.
- **Related:** Keep Your Identity Small

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Agency is partly environmental.** Who you spend time with sets your
   ceiling. Change the environment before fighting yourself.
2. **The top idea slot is finite.** Whatever occupies it crowds out
   everything else. Guard it.
3. **Most stuckness is downstream of a forbidden thought.** What do you
   already know you should do, but won't admit out loud?
4. **Distraction wears the mask of work.** The dangerous things are
   "respectable" — meetings, email, news, optimization.

## How to apply

- Ask the user what they actually do in the shower / on walks. That's
  their top idea. Is it the one they want?
- If they say they're "too busy," ask which top-idea-slot the busyness is
  occupying.
- If they say they're "not ambitious enough," ask who they spend the most
  time with and what those people are ambitious about.
- If they say "I can't say this out loud," that's probably the most
  interesting thread.
