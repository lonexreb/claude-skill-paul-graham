# Startup Ideas — Finding, Validating, Schlep Blindness

PG's core claim: startup ideas don't come from "thinking up startup ideas."
They come from noticing things that should exist, in domains you already
inhabit. Trying to be a startup idea generator usually produces bad ideas
that sound good.

## Core essays

### "How to Get Startup Ideas" (2012)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/startupideas.html
- **Thesis:** Don't think up startup ideas — *notice* them. Live in the
  future, then build what's missing. The best filter for a real idea is
  whether you and your friends would actually use it.
- **When this applies:** Default essay for any "how do I find a startup
  idea" question. Pull this first.
- **Related:** Schlep Blindness, Organic Startup Ideas

### "Schlep Blindness" (2012)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/schlep.html
- **Thesis:** The best startup ideas are protected from competition by
  being unpleasant to start. Founders unconsciously filter out ideas that
  involve hard work, regulation, sales, or unsexy domains — leaving them
  for whoever is willing to look past the schlep.
- **When this applies:** User has lots of "fun" ideas that all sound like
  consumer apps. Or user dismissed a real idea because "ugh, that would
  involve sales calls."
- **Related:** Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas

### "Organic Startup Ideas" (2010)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/organic.html
- **Thesis:** The best ideas grow out of your own life. The worst come
  from trying to think of ideas you could sell. Test: would you keep
  working on this if it didn't make any money?
- **When this applies:** User asks "is this idea good?" — flip it: is it
  *theirs*? Did it come from a real itch they have?
- **Related:** How to Get Startup Ideas

### "Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas" (2012)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/ambitious.html
- **Thesis:** Big ideas seem so hard they scare founders away — which is
  exactly why they remain unclaimed. The defense is to start with a small
  thing inside the big thing.
- **When this applies:** User thinks their idea is "too ambitious" or
  thinks they're "not the kind of person" to work on a big problem.
- **Related:** Schlep Blindness

### "The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn" (2006)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/startuplessons.html
- **Thesis:** A list of counterintuitive truths founders resist —
  including that release early, keep pumping out features, fear the right
  things, and commitment is a self-fulfilling prophecy.
- **When this applies:** User is over-polishing, hiding their product,
  scared of competitors, or generally trying to be a real-grown-up-company
  before they have customers.
- **Related:** Do Things that Don't Scale, Make Something People Want

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Notice, don't generate.** Ideas you "think up" are usually too
   abstract. Ideas you *bump into* are real.
2. **Schlep is a moat.** If the idea is fun and easy, someone else
   already started it.
3. **Authenticity test.** Would you keep building this for free? If yes,
   it's organic. If no, you're chasing optics.
4. **Wedge into ambition.** The right big idea has a small starting
   point. Find the wedge, not the master plan.

## How to apply

- When the user describes an idea, ask: *did you think this up, or did
  you notice it?* If they thought it up, ask what real problem they have
  that no one has solved.
- Ask what they avoid working on because "it would be a slog." That's
  often where the moat is.
- If they say their idea is "too small," ask what big system the small
  idea would be the wedge into.
- If they say their idea is "too big," ask what tiny version they could
  ship in two weeks.
