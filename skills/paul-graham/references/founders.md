# Founders — Traits, Determination, Co-founders

PG has spent more time evaluating founders than almost anyone. The pattern
he describes is not "smart" or "technical" — it's a specific cluster:
relentlessly resourceful, determined, flexible, and not afraid of looking
stupid.

## Core essays

### "What We Look for in Founders" (2010)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/founders.html
- **Thesis:** YC looks for five qualities: determination, flexibility,
  imagination, naughtiness, and friendship (with co-founders).
  Determination is the most important. Smart matters less than
  determined.
- **When this applies:** User asks "am I founder material?", "what makes
  a great founder?", or evaluates a potential co-founder.
- **Related:** Relentlessly Resourceful, Determination, Mean People Fail

### "Relentlessly Resourceful" (2009)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/relres.html
- **Thesis:** The shortest description of a good founder is "relentlessly
  resourceful." Resourceful alone is not enough; relentless alone is not
  enough. The combination is what gets startups through impossible
  situations.
- **When this applies:** User is stuck and looking for "the right
  process" or "the standard playbook." There isn't one. The trait is
  improvisation under pressure.
- **Related:** Determination, Do Things that Don't Scale

### "Determination" (2009)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/determination.html
- **Thesis:** Determination has two parts — willfulness and discipline.
  Willfulness without discipline is stubbornness on the wrong things.
  Discipline without willfulness is a salaried employee. You need both,
  and the right kind of willfulness is *focused* — pointed at one thing
  for a long time.
- **When this applies:** User questions whether they're "tough enough,"
  or seems to confuse determination with rigidity.
- **Related:** Relentlessly Resourceful

### "Mean People Fail" (2014)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/mean.html
- **Thesis:** Mean founders fail more often than nice ones. Meanness
  signals insecurity, and insecurity makes you optimize for status games
  rather than building. Also: mean people can't recruit great people.
- **When this applies:** User is asking "do you have to be a jerk to
  succeed?" or describes a co-founder who's brilliant but cruel. The
  answer is no, and that co-founder is a long-term risk.
- **Related:** What We Look for in Founders

### "Why to Not Not Start a Startup" (2007)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/notnot.html
- **Thesis:** The classic objections to starting a startup — "I'm too
  young," "I don't have an idea," "I don't have a co-founder," "the
  economy is bad," "I'd be giving up a great job" — are mostly
  rationalizations. PG walks through each and dismantles it.
- **When this applies:** User has a specific reason they "can't" start a
  company. Identify which objection it is and pull this essay's response
  to that one.
- **Related:** Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Determination > intelligence.** YC's data on this is unambiguous.
   The smartest founders aren't the ones who win.
2. **"Relentlessly resourceful" is the test.** Not "experienced," not
   "credentialed."
3. **Mean is a bug, not a feature.** It correlates with failure, not
   success.
4. **Most objections are dressed-up fear.** "I don't have an idea" is
   rarely the real reason someone isn't starting.

## How to apply

- When evaluating a founder/co-founder, ask: *have they ever gotten
  through an impossible-seeming situation by improvising?* That's the
  signal.
- When the user lists reasons they can't start, push: *is this the real
  reason, or is it the acceptable reason?*
- When the user worries about being "too nice," reassure them. Mean is
  not an asset.
- When the user is rigid about a strategy that isn't working, separate
  determination (the goal) from stubbornness (the tactic).
