# Growth — First Users, Doing Things That Don't Scale

PG's growth essays are the antidote to founders who confuse "having a
plan" with "having traction." The core idea: in the early days, do
unscalable things to get and keep users. Growth is the only feature
that matters.

## Core essays

### "Startup = Growth" (2012)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/growth.html
- **Thesis:** A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Not all new
  companies are startups — most small businesses aren't trying to grow
  fast and that's fine. But if you call yourself a startup, growth rate
  is the *only* metric that matters, because everything else follows
  from it.
- **When this applies:** User confuses revenue with growth, or treats a
  lifestyle business as a startup, or doesn't know what their growth
  rate is.
- **Related:** Default Alive or Default Dead, Do Things that Don't Scale

### "Do Things that Don't Scale" (2013)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/ds.html
- **Thesis:** Most founders ask "how do we scale this?" too early. In
  the beginning you have to do unscalable things — recruit users
  manually, give absurdly good customer service, hand-deliver the
  product. Growth comes from intensity per user, not from automation.
- **When this applies:** User wants to "set up a funnel" before they
  have any users, or refuses to email customers individually, or thinks
  doing things by hand is beneath them.
- **Related:** Make Something People Want

### "The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups" (2006)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/startupmistakes.html
- **Thesis:** A catalog of common failure modes — single founder, bad
  location, marginal niche, derivative idea, obstinacy, hiring bad
  programmers, choosing the wrong platform, slowness in launching,
  launching too early, having no specific user in mind, raising too
  little, spending too much, raising too much, poor investor management,
  sacrificing users to (supposed) profit, not wanting to get your hands
  dirty, fights between founders, half-hearted effort.
- **When this applies:** User is having trouble and wants a diagnostic
  checklist. Walk through with them.
- **Related:** What We Look for in Founders

### "Default Alive or Default Dead" (2015)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/aord.html
- **Thesis:** Every startup is either default alive (will reach
  profitability before money runs out, given current growth) or default
  dead (won't). Many founders don't know which they are, and that's the
  problem. The question must be answerable.
- **When this applies:** User is fundraising, planning hires, or anxious
  about runway. Make them compute the answer.
- **Related:** Startup = Growth, How to Raise Money

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Growth rate is the only metric.** If you don't have weekly growth
   numbers, you don't know how your startup is doing.
2. **Manual > automated, early on.** The best founders go meet users in
   person. Stripe famously installed their product for the first users.
3. **Default alive is a question with a numeric answer.** If you don't
   know yours, compute it before anything else.
4. **Most "mistakes" are predictable.** PG's list of 18 covers most
   ways startups die. Run the checklist.

## How to apply

- Always ask: *what's your weekly growth rate?* If they can't answer,
  that's the first thing to fix.
- If they're trying to "scale" with 3 users, redirect them to manual
  recruitment.
- If they're worried about runway, ask: *default alive or default dead?*
  Make them compute it.
- If they're stuck and don't know why, run them through the 18 mistakes
  list. One of them usually lights up.
