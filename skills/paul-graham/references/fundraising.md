# Fundraising — Investors, Term Sheets, Conviction

PG's fundraising essays are mostly about not letting fundraising destroy
the startup. The two failure modes: founders who can't raise because they
can't convince anyone, and founders who raise so much it kills the
discipline that made the startup work.

## Core essays

### "How to Convince Investors" (2013)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/convince.html
- **Thesis:** Investors are convinced when *you* are convinced. You can't
  fake conviction; you have to actually be convinced — which means
  knowing your market and your numbers in depth. Three things investors
  evaluate: are the founders impressive, is the idea good, is there
  evidence (growth) it's working.
- **When this applies:** User is preparing to pitch, struggling to raise,
  or asking "what do investors want to see?"
- **Related:** How to Raise Money, Fundraising Survival Guide

### "How to Raise Money" (2013)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/fr.html
- **Thesis:** The mechanics: start when you have a clear story, do it in
  parallel (not sequentially), have a specific ask, optimize for getting
  the first commit, and stop when you have enough. Don't optimize for
  valuation at the cost of speed or signal.
- **When this applies:** User is *in* a fundraising round and making
  tactical decisions. This is the playbook.
- **Related:** How to Convince Investors

### "Fundraising Survival Guide" (2009)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/fundraising.html
- **Thesis:** Fundraising will kill your startup if you let it consume
  more than half your attention. Run it in compressed bursts. Pick a
  founder to run it; the other(s) keep building. Don't try to fundraise
  while shipping.
- **When this applies:** User says "I've been raising for 6 months,"
  or both co-founders are running pitches and the product is stalling.
- **Related:** Do Things that Don't Scale

### "The Hacker's Guide to Investors" (2007)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/guidetoinvestors.html
- **Thesis:** Investor psychology decoded for technical founders.
  Investors are pattern-matchers, deeply herd-driven, and afraid of
  *looking* wrong more than being wrong. Knowing this changes how you
  talk to them.
- **When this applies:** User is bewildered by investor behavior — a VC
  who was excited went cold, or a "soft yes" that never closes. Help
  them read the underlying signals.
- **Related:** How to Convince Investors

### "The Future of Startup Funding" (2010)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/future.html
- **Thesis:** The shift from a few big rounds to many small ones, and
  the rise of "super angels" / seed funds, means founders have more
  leverage than they think. Understand the structure of the funding
  market to negotiate within it.
- **When this applies:** User is trying to figure out *which type* of
  investor to target — angel, seed fund, multi-stage VC.
- **Related:** How to Raise Money

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Conviction is the asset.** You can't pitch what you don't believe.
   If you can't convince yourself, fix that before pitching.
2. **Run fundraising in parallel.** Sequential meetings with one VC at
   a time are how rounds die.
3. **Time-box it.** Fundraising is corrosive — it competes with the
   building that justifies the fundraise.
4. **Investors are herding animals.** First commit unlocks the rest.
   Optimize for the first yes, not the highest valuation.

## How to apply

- Before any pitch prep, ask: *what's your one-sentence reason this will
  work?* If they hesitate or hedge, that's the gap.
- If they're stuck in a slow round, ask: are you running this in
  parallel, or one at a time?
- If both founders are pitching, redirect one back to the product.
- If they're being told "great, let's talk again in a few weeks,"
  decode it: that's usually a soft no.
