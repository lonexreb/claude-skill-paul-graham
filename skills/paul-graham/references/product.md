# Product, Taste, Building

PG's product writing centers on one phrase: *make something people want*.
Everything else — distribution, branding, growth tactics — is downstream.
If users aren't pulling, no amount of pushing fixes it.

## Core essays

### "Make Something People Want" (2011)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/good.html
- **Thesis:** The unofficial YC motto. Most startups fail because they
  made something nobody wanted, even if they had a plan, a team, and a
  pitch. The check on this: are users coming back, telling their
  friends, getting angry when the product breaks?
- **When this applies:** User is deep in "should I do marketing now?"
  but their retention curve is flat. Or user has a polished product
  with no users.
- **Related:** Do Things that Don't Scale, Startup = Growth

### "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" (2009)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html
- **Thesis:** Makers and managers operate on different time. A manager's
  day is sliced into one-hour slots. A maker needs half-day blocks. A
  single mid-afternoon meeting can destroy a maker's whole day. Founders
  who switch between modes badly lose enormous productivity.
- **When this applies:** User is a technical founder feeling drained by
  meetings, or a non-technical founder confused why their engineer is
  "always grumpy about meetings."
- **Related:** Disconnecting Distraction

### "Taste for Makers" (2002)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/taste.html
- **Thesis:** Good design — across writing, code, products, anything
  made — has shared properties: simplicity, suggestiveness, redesigns,
  the right level of abstraction. Taste isn't subjective the way people
  claim; it's a learnable signal.
- **When this applies:** User asks how to develop taste, how to decide
  between two design directions, or whether something is "good."
- **Related:** Make Something People Want

### "The Power of the Marginal" (2006)
- **URL:** https://www.paulgraham.com/marginal.html
- **Thesis:** Outsiders have a structural advantage: they have nothing
  to lose, no reputation to protect, and no incumbent investment to
  defend. The marginal position lets you try things insiders can't.
- **When this applies:** User feels they're "not credentialed enough"
  or "not the obvious person" to attack a problem. That's often an
  asset.
- **Related:** Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas, What You Can't Say

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Want is the test.** If users don't want it badly, nothing else
   you do matters.
2. **Maker time is sacred.** Defend half-day blocks. Don't let meetings
   fragment them.
3. **Taste is signal, not noise.** The properties of good design are
   learnable and convergent across domains.
4. **Outsider status is leverage.** "I'm not the obvious person" is
   often why you can do this.

## How to apply

- Before product strategy, ask: *do your users come back unprompted?*
  If not, retention is the only problem.
- For a technical founder drowning in meetings, ask: *how many
  half-day maker blocks did you have this week?*
- For a "should we add this feature?" question, ask: *what would the
  best designers you admire add or cut here?* Taste is contagious.
- For "I'm not the right person to do this," respond with the
  marginal advantage frame.
